Digital Literacy Skills

To become digitally literate, you must know about technology and how to use it correctly. This includes knowing how to get credible information from websites, reliable methods of communication, and how to navigate your own computer, as well as other essential skills. These and more are outlined in the text below.

Reliable Information

As the internet is more and more open to everyone, there has been and continues to be a rise in spreading misinformation, or information that is heavily biased or one-sided. To make sure that the information you need is free from bias, we must ask ourselves: "Who made this and why?" Knowing the purpose of the content and who made it helps to consider possible sources of bias. A good strategy to use when researching is to read "laterally". Lateral reading is when you look at multiple different sources to gain information instead of a single source. By considering multiple viewpoints, it makes the information you read about more impartial. It is good practice to verify the information you gain with other sources to prove/disprove it, and to look to sites which are known to be trusted (.gov or .edu sites, to name a few).

Communication

A very important part of being digitally literate is having the ability to share information. Whether you need to send an email to a colleague from Outlook, or share your findings in a video through YouTube, communication is a vital aspect of digital literacy. You need to know what software to use for which purpose (For example, using the Office 365 Suite's apps to present notes, slideshows, and spreadsheets, or Google Drive for storage and easy sharing). There are so many ways to communicate information, which means it is quite important you know what to use and when.

ISTE Standards for Students

As internet tools and gadgets are invented at an exponential pace, it is up to educators to help provide guidance and understanding as students build the dimension of their digital footprint. According to ISTE standards for students, digital citizenship means students understand human, cultural, and societal issues related to technology and practice legal and ethical behavior.
a. Advocate and practice safe, legal, and responsible use of information and technology
b. Exhibit a positive attitude toward using technology that supports collaboration, learning, and productivity
c. Demonstrate personal responsibility for lifelong learning
d. Exhibit leadership for digital citizenship
